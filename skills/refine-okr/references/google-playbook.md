# Google OKR Playbook

Source: https://www.whatmatters.com/resources/google-okr-playbook

## Good objectives

An objective should express a goal and intent that is aggressive yet realistic. It must be tangible, objective, and unambiguous — it should be obvious at the end of the cycle whether it was achieved. It must deliver clear business or end-user value and fit on a single line.

Test: "If we achieve this, what gets better?" If the answer is unclear, the objective is framed as an activity rather than an outcome.

## Good key results

Key results must express measurable milestones that, if achieved, will advance the objective. They must:

- Describe **outcomes**, not activities — avoid verbs like "consult", "help", "analyze", "implement"
- Include a specific numeric target with an unambiguous definition and a real deadline
- Include evidence of completion that is credible and verifiable

**Sufficiency test:** Scoring 1.0 on every key result must guarantee that the objective is fully met. If doubt remains after all KRs score 1.0, the KRs are necessary but not sufficient — revise them.

## Scoring

| Type | Expected score | Interpretation |
|------|---------------|----------------|
| Committed | 1.0 | Any score below 1.0 requires a postmortem on planning or execution |
| Aspirational | ~0.7 average | High variance expected; below 0.4 is failing |

## Rules of thumb

- OKRs that don't require genuine priority shifts are business-as-usual — remove or elevate them
- Commitments + aspirations combined should exceed available team capacity
- Misclassifying type (committed vs aspirational) creates either complacency or defensiveness
