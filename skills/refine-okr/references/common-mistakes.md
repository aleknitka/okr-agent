# Common OKR Mistakes

Source: https://www.whatmatters.com/faqs/common-okr-mistakes

## 1. Misclassified type
Treating a committed OKR as aspirational leads teams to accept partial achievement as fine. Treating an aspirational OKR as committed creates anxiety and defensiveness. Each OKR's type must match how it will be graded and resourced.

## 2. Business-as-usual OKRs
Goals the team would achieve without changing anything are not OKRs — they are routine operations. OKRs must require a genuine shift in priorities. Ask: would achieving this at 1.0 create tangible value that wouldn't otherwise exist?

## 3. Timid aspirational OKRs
Aspirational targets must start from a vision of an ideal future state, not from a small increment over current capability. The right framing: "What could our customers' world look like if freed from current constraints?" If the goal doesn't feel uncomfortable, it isn't aspirational.

## 4. Sandbagging
See [sandbagging.md](sandbagging.md) for a full treatment. Short version: committed OKRs should credibly consume most available team resources. If a team consistently hits all goals with capacity to spare, targets are too low.

## 5. Low-value objectives
An objective is low-value if completing it at full score produces no direct end-user or economic benefit. Internal activity (deploying a system, running a process) does not qualify unless the outcome for users or the business is explicit.

## 6. Insufficient key results
Key results must be collectively sufficient to guarantee the objective — not just necessary. If scoring 1.0 on every KR still leaves the objective in doubt, additional or stronger KRs are needed.
